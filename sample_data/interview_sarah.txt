Interview Transcript — Teacher Professional Development Study
Participant: Sarah, High School Science Teacher, 15 years experience
Date: March 2024, Duration: 45 minutes

I: Tell me about your experience with the new curriculum standards.

SARAH: Honestly, it's been overwhelming. They just dropped these new standards on us at the beginning of the year with almost no training. We had one half-day workshop where someone read PowerPoint slides to us, and that was supposed to prepare us to completely change how we teach.

I: How did that make you feel?

SARAH: Frustrated. Really frustrated. I've been teaching for fifteen years. I know my content. But these standards want us to integrate all this technology and project-based learning, and nobody showed us how. It's like they assume we already know how to do it, or maybe they just don't care about giving us proper support.

I: Can you describe how you've been adapting?

SARAH: Well, I've had to figure most of it out on my own. I spent my entire winter break watching YouTube videos and reading blog posts about project-based learning. That's not professional development — that's survival. A few of us started meeting after school on Wednesdays, just informally, sharing what's working and what isn't. That's been the most helpful thing, actually — learning from each other rather than from the district.

I: Tell me more about those Wednesday meetings.

SARAH: There are about six of us now, all science teachers. We call it our "sanity circle." We share lesson plans, troubleshoot technology issues, and honestly, sometimes we just vent. Last week Maria showed us this amazing way to use data collection apps with student lab work, and it was more useful than anything the district has ever provided. We're building something from the ground up because the system didn't give us what we needed.

I: How has this affected your students?

SARAH: That's the hard part. My students are suffering because I'm still learning. The first semester was rough — I tried implementing project-based learning and it was a disaster. The kids were confused, I was confused, and their test scores dropped. Some parents complained. My principal pulled me aside and said I needed to "get it together," which was incredibly demoralizing when I was already working 70-hour weeks trying to make this work.

I: What changed in the second semester?

SARAH: I stopped trying to do everything at once. I picked two things to focus on — using technology for data collection and having students do one collaborative project per unit. That was manageable. And the Wednesday group helped me refine my approach before I took it into the classroom. My students are responding better now. They actually like the projects. One kid told me, "This is the first time science has felt real." That made everything worth it.

I: How would you describe the relationship between teachers and administration through this process?

SARAH: There's a real disconnect. Administration talks about innovation and twenty-first century learning, but they don't walk the walk. They mandate change without providing resources. They evaluate us on the new standards but didn't train us on them. It feels like we're set up to fail. The teachers who are succeeding are the ones who took initiative on their own — not because of the system, but despite it.

I: Is there anything else you'd like to share?

SARAH: I just wish someone would listen to teachers before making these decisions. We're not resistant to change — I actually think the new standards are good ideas. But the implementation has been terrible. If they had involved teachers in the planning, given us a year to prepare, provided real training and ongoing support, this could have been transformative. Instead, it's been traumatic. The good news is that what we're building in our Wednesday group — that grassroots professional learning — that's actually powerful. It just shouldn't have had to come from us.
